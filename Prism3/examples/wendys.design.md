## Overview

## Brand Identity
**Brand:** Wendy's
**Tagline:** Quality Is Our Recipe® / "Keepin' It Fresh"
**Positioning:** A quality-first quick-service restaurant brand that competes on fresh-never-frozen food, sass, and confident personality — not just speed or price.

## Brand Personality
**Traits:** Sassy, confident, irreverent, fresh, quality-obsessed
**Archetype:** The Jester (with a side of The Hero — sass + standards)
The Wendy's brand voice is the friend who roasts the competition and lets you in on the joke, but takes its food deadly seriously. Design should feel high-energy and unpretentious — bold red blocking, no decoration for decoration's sake, photography (or product) leads. Never corporate, never trying too hard.

## Audience
**Primary:** Fast-food consumers ages 18-35 ordering on mobile — value quality and personality over pure convenience, high social-media literacy, short attention spans.
**Secondary:** Family/lunchtime customers ordering pickup or delivery from the restaurant via desktop.
**Audience context:** Mobile-first ordering. Often on the move (commute, break). Stop-the-scroll energy required for marketing surfaces; quiet efficiency required for transactional surfaces.
**Key use cases:** Find a nearby Wendy's, order food for pickup or delivery, customize menu items, manage Wendy's Rewards, scan QR for in-store ordering, redeem offers.

## Visual Language
**Direction:** High-energy, photography-forward, bold red color blocking on white. The cameo (Wendy portrait) is the brand's anchor. Confident hierarchy, generous whitespace, modern type — a Wendy's screen should feel obviously Wendy's at a glance, not a generic QSR template.
**Principles:**
1. **Food (or product) is the hero** — every layout puts the menu item or offer front and center.
2. **Bold, not loud** — confident use of the red on white, no rainbow palettes, no decoration competing with content.
3. **Personality through copy** — let the voice carry the wit; visuals stay disciplined.
4. **Mobile-native** — design for thumb-reach phones first; desktop is a scaled-up phone.
5. **Quality cues, never cute** — stay on the right side of "modern-classic"; avoid trendy gimmicks.

**Signature elements:** The Wendy cameo (her redheaded portrait), the Wave script wordmark, the square hamburger motif, brand red blocks on white grounds.

## Competitive Context
**Differentiation:** Personality-driven where McDonald's and Burger King lean on scale or flame-grilling. Wendy's wins on wit, food quality cues, and social-media-native voice.
**Avoid resemblance to:** McDonald's golden-arch yellow/red warmth (Wendy's red is cooler and standalone), Burger King's flame aesthetic, Chick-fil-A's earnest small-town vibe, Shake Shack's craft/upscale-casual look.
**Aesthetic anti-patterns:** NOT corporate minimalist (too sterile), NOT fast-casual upscale (too serious), NOT retro diner (too nostalgic), NOT generic flat (too forgettable), NOT glassmorphic, NOT neobrutalist. Wendy's is modern + energetic — never stuffy.

## Brand self-test (run before presenting work)
1. Could this screen belong to a competitor? (should be NO)
2. Is the food (or product) front and center, not buried below the fold?
3. Does the copy have personality — would you smile reading it?
4. Is the red Wendy's red — warm, not aggressive, not pink, not orange?
5. Is the cameo or Wave wordmark present in header/footer/watermark?
6. Does the layout feel confident — strong hierarchy, no clutter, no rainbow palette?
7. Could this headline work as a tweet? (tone check)
8. Are interactive elements obvious — no mystery-meat navigation?
9. Does it feel mobile-native, not desktop-shrunk?
10. Would a 22-year-old scrolling on their phone stop and engage? (energy check)

## Philosophy
**Approach:** Red is the brand. The Wendy's red carries the entire visual identity — everything else exists to support it. We do not dilute the red with competing brand colors, gradients, or decorative palettes. Backgrounds default to white; the red appears as accents, CTAs, and the cameo.
**Palette strategy:** Monochromatic primary (red, with darker shades for state) over a neutral gray scale. A blue secondary exists for cause work and the Frosty sub-brand. A crimson tertiary adds depth in graphics. Semantic colors (success/warning/error/info) are distinct from brand red so urgency reads clearly.

## Application Context

**Background rules:**
- Page backgrounds: `color-white` is the default. `color-neutral-100` for secondary surfaces.
- Section backgrounds: White or neutral-100. The red appears as a *contained* color block (banner, hero card, promotional strip), not full-bleed multi-screen sections.
- Brand-primary backgrounds (`color-primary`): contained accent areas only — CTAs, badges, alert banners, hero color-blocks. Pair with `color-text-onBrand-default` (#FFFFFF) text.
- Inverse / dark surfaces: `color-neutral-1100` (#191A1B), used for footer or hero overlays.

**Text color rules:**
- Primary text: `color-neutral-1100` on light, `color-neutral-100` on dark.
- Secondary text: `color-neutral-800` on light, `color-neutral-200` on dark.
- Brand-color text: `color-primary-dark` (#9E0D24) is the smallest-allowed primary brand text. Brand-primary at `#C8102E` only at ≥18px bold or ≥24px (large-text WCAG AA).
- Links: `color-secondary` (#0077A3); inverse links are `color-white`.
- Disabled text: `color-neutral-600`.
- Validation text: use the matching semantic token (`color-error`, `color-warning`, `color-success`).

**Accent usage:**
- Primary CTAs: `color-primary` filled with white text.
- Secondary CTAs: white-fill, `color-primary-dark` border + label.
- Active nav items / tabs: red underline or red label on white.
- Icons: red brand-icons follow the same large-only rule — use `color-primary-dark` or darker for small icons.
- Badges, tags, deal callouts: red is acceptable here; this is what the red is *for*.

**Combinations:**
- Approved: red + white + neutral text. Red + crimson (#9D2235) for graphic depth in marketing.
- Avoid: red + yellow combos ("we stay away from red and yellow combos because… gross." — Merch 2.0).
- Avoid: brand-red and error-red on the same screen at the same time — they read identically and confuse meaning. Pick one signal.

## Technical Constraints
**Contrast rules:**
- Body text: 4.5:1 minimum (WCAG AA). All `color-neutral-700` and darker on white meet this.
- Large text (≥18px bold or ≥24px): 3:1 minimum.
- `color-primary` (#C8102E) on white: ~4.6:1 — passes AA for **large text only**. For small text use `color-primary-dark` (#9E0D24) which clears the small-text bar.
- UI component contrast (icons, focus rings): 3:1 minimum against background.

**Restrictions:**
- Never use `color-primary` for error semantics — use `color-error`. Brand red and error red look related but communicate different things.
- Maximum 3 brand colors per component (excluding neutrals). Most components use 1.
- No raw hex/RGB in implementation — always reference a token.
- No gradient swaps of brand colors. The red is flat.

## Usage by Medium

**Website / Web App (order.wendys.com):**
- White backgrounds dominate. Brand red appears as accents, CTAs, badges, and active states.
- Live site uses #B0161F as a primary brand red surface (slightly different from the DS-defined #C8102E and the brand-guide-defined PMS 186 #CD2028) — see `conflicts.md`.
- Body font defaults to `Roboto, Arial, sans-serif`; headlines and emphasis to `wendysFresh`.

**Print / Campaign:**
- Lead with red — campaign materials are color-forward (red-on-white, white-on-red, full-bleed red is allowed in print where it isn't in web UI).
- CMYK conversions of PMS 186 / 299 / 201 used; hex values are digital-only.

**Social Media:**
- Bolder red blocking acceptable. Frequent use of GT Walsheim Ultra Bold campaign type on red backgrounds.
- Avoid the red+yellow combo per merch guidance.

**CRM / Email:**
- Simplified palette. White background, red CTA, neutral-1100 body text. Skip secondary blue unless the email is Frosty- or cause-related.

## Dark Mode
**Strategy:** The Fresh DS Foundations defines `inverse` variants for text/icon/border tokens (e.g., `color-text-primary-inverse` → #F0F1F1). Use these for dark contexts (dark hero overlays, footer). A full dark-mode theme is not currently shipped; treat dark surfaces as exceptions, not a parallel theme.
**Token mapping:** Inverse tokens swap text/icon/border to lighter neutrals; backgrounds invert to `color-neutral-1100` (#191A1B), `color-neutral-1000` (#2B2D2E), or `color-neutral-900` (#3F4143).

## Typography

## Philosophy
**Approach:** Type carries the brand's confident personality. Wendy's display work is heavy and unmissable; UI body work is plain, readable, and gets out of the way. Big black-weight headlines + neutral body type = swagger without showboating.
**Pairing rationale:** Two families. **Wendy's Fresh** (custom display) provides the brand voice on every Mega/Display/Title/Button typeset. **Roboto** (Google Fonts, ubiquitous) covers all body text and captions — chosen for screen legibility and global availability. The contrast (display-bold + body-neutral) lets the headlines do the personality work while body copy stays scannable. Print/campaign work uses **Intro** and **GT Walsheim Ultra Bold** instead — see usage rules.

## Font Families
**Primary (display):** wendysFresh
- Weights: 700 (Bold), 900 (Black)
- Role: Headlines (Mega/Display), titles, buttons
- Source: Self-hosted custom font (via `--font-wendys-fresh` CSS variable on order.wendys.com)
- Fallback: `"wendysFresh Fallback", Roboto, Arial, sans-serif`

**Body:** Roboto
- Weights: 400 (Regular), 500 (Medium — captions only), 700 (Bold)
- Role: Body text, captions, all UI prose, form labels
- Source: Google Fonts (or self-hosted)
- Fallback: `"Roboto Fallback", Arial, sans-serif`

**Print/Campaign (not for web):**
- **Intro** family (Light, Regular, SemiBold, Bold, Black + italics) — Wendy's master brand body font for printed materials.
- **GT Walsheim Ultra Bold** — current campaign/headline font for merch and out-of-home.

**Loading strategy:** `font-display: swap` for both web families. Preload `wendysFresh` since it appears above the fold on most pages.

**Fallback stack:** All web typesets fall back through the Wendy's-defined fallbacks → `Roboto, Arial, sans-serif` → system sans-serif, in that order.

**Weight restrictions:**

- Never use wendysFresh for body paragraphs — too heavy for sustained reading.

## Usage Rules
**Minimum sizes:**
- Body text: 14px (Body-S). 16px (Body-M) preferred.
- Caption: 11px (Caption-S). Anything smaller is illegible on mobile.

**Maximum line length:** 65 characters for body text; 75 for `body-s`. Long lines kill readability — wrap or split.

**Hierarchy rules:**
- Establish hierarchy with size + weight. Color alone is not hierarchy.
- Headings always use `wendysFresh`. Body always uses Roboto. No mixing within a sentence.
- Brand-color text (red) only at `≥18px bold` or `≥24px` per WCAG AA contrast — never on small body or captions. Use `color-primary-dark` for small brand-color text.

**OpenType features:** `font-feature-settings: "kern" 1, "liga" 1` on all text. No fancy alternates by default.

## Philosophy
**Density approach:** Balanced — Wendy's UI breathes on marketing surfaces (hero, menu cards) and tightens on transactional surfaces (checkout, order summary). Whitespace signals confidence; cramped layouts feel cheap, which contradicts the "quality" pillar.
**Spacing principle:** Always use a token. No magic numbers, no inline pixel values. When in doubt, go up one step — the brand can afford the room.

## Application Rules
**Component internal:**
- Buttons: `space-s` vertical, `space-l` horizontal (matches the observed `12px 30px` and `12px 10px` pairs on order.wendys.com).
- Cards: `space-l` padding default; `space-xl` for large feature cards.
- Form fields: `space-s` vertical padding, `space-m` gap between fields.
- Modal/sheet body: `space-l` padding.

**Component external:**
- Related components: `space-m` to `space-l`.
- Unrelated components: `space-xl` to `space-2xl`.
- Section gaps: `space-2xl` mobile, `space-4xl` desktop.

**Page-level:**
- Container max-width: 1200px (typical). Container horizontal padding: `space-m` mobile, `space-xl` desktop.
- Section vertical gaps: `space-3xl` mobile, `space-4xl` desktop.
- Header vertical padding: `space-m`.

**Responsive adjustments:** Spacing tokens above `space-xl` reduce by one step on viewports < 768px (e.g., `space-4xl` → `space-3xl`). Internal component spacing stays fixed.

## Anti-patterns
- Never use raw pixel values — always a scale token.
- Don't mix `space-2xs` and `space-xs` in the same row of elements — pick one rhythm.
- Section spacing below `space-2xl` makes the page feel cramped — Wendy's needs room to breathe.
- Don't compress hero padding below `space-3xl` even on mobile; the headline needs air.

| `radius-3xl` | 24px | Marquee panels, featured campaign cards. |
| `radius-rounded` | 9999px | Pills, avatar circles, round icon buttons. |

**Default radius for interactive elements:** `radius-m` (8px) — overridden by `radius-rounded` for pill-shaped CTAs (which appear on the live site for primary actions).

## Borders
**Border widths:**
- `border-default`: 1px — standard borders, inputs, cards.
- `border-thick`: 2px — focus rings, active selection, emphasis.

**Border colors:** `color-border-secondary-default` (`color-neutral-300` / #CBCBCC) for default borders. `color-border-primary-default` (`color-neutral-700` / #63666A) for stronger emphasis. `color-border-brand-primary-default` (`color-primary-dark` / #9E0D24) for brand-active borders.

**Border usage:**
- Default cards: 1px border (`color-neutral-300`), no shadow.
- Inputs: 1px border in default state; 2px brand-primary border on focus.
- Floating elements (dropdowns, modals, popovers): no border, use shadow instead.
- Section dividers: 1px `color-neutral-200` horizontal rule.

## Elevation / Shadows
| Token | Value | Use |
|-------|-------|-----|
| `shadow-flat` | none | Default state for static cards and layout containers. |
| `shadow-sm` | `0 1px 4px -1px rgba(0,0,0,0.3)` | Subtle lift: sticky headers, hover state on interactive cards. |
| `shadow-md` | `0 4px 8px 0 rgba(0,0,0,0.2)` | Dropdowns, popovers, sticky bottom bag (observed on order.wendys.com). |
| `shadow-lg` | `0 0 18px 0 rgba(0,0,0,0.2)` | Modals, dialogs, full-screen overlays. |

**Elevation rules:**
- Static, in-flow cards: no shadow. Use a 1px border for separation.
- Hover state on interactive cards: `shadow-sm` lift, no other change.
- Floating elements (always visually detached): `shadow-md`.
- Modal/dialog: `shadow-lg`.
- Never stack shadows on the same element.

## Anti-patterns
- No inset shadows — they don't match brand.
- Don't combine thick (2px+) borders and shadows on the same element — pick one separation strategy.
- No gradient borders.
- Avoid `radius-rounded` on rectangular content containers — pills are for buttons, badges, avatars, and small UI; not whole cards.
- No multi-layered glassmorphic translucency — Wendy's is solid color, not vapor.

## Do's and Don'ts

## Rainbow Brand Palette
**Description:** Using brand red, brand blue, crimson, and semantic colors all on the same screen as decoration. Wendy's color strategy is monochromatic primary (red) on white. Multiple brand colors competing for attention dilute the red and feel generic-QSR.
**Example:** A menu page where category headers are red, the hero card is blue, the deal badge is crimson, and the CTAs are red — all visible at once.
**Instead:** Red is the lead. Use the blue only when contextually appropriate (Frosty sub-brand, links, cause work). Crimson is graphic depth, used sparingly. Most screens should read as red + neutral.
**Severity:** error

## Red + Yellow combinations
**Description:** Using brand red alongside any saturated yellow. Borrowed from Merch 2.0 — "stay away from red and yellow combos because… gross." Reads as McDonald's, not Wendy's.
**Instead:** Red + white + neutrals. If you need a contrast accent, use the brand blue or a neutral gray.
**Severity:** error

## Brand Red as Full-Page Background
**Description:** Using `color-primary` as a full-bleed page background or massive multi-screen section. The red is meant to anchor and accent — at full-page scale it stops feeling intentional and starts feeling like a banner ad.
**Example:** A hero section that's 100vh of solid red.
**Instead:** Red appears in contained color-blocks: hero card, CTA, banner strip, product callout. Default page background is white.
**Severity:** warning (ok in print/marketing campaigns; not in product UI)

## Cameo or Wave alterations
**Description:** Recoloring, rotating, skewing, animating the cameo, adding outlines, drop shadows, holding shapes, or cropping the Wave wordmark. The brand guide is unambiguous: "Drop shadows were cool back in nineteen ninety never. Outlines? You guessed it. No. Rotating is a definite no-no. Don't skew it. We know it's tempting, but don't change or animate the cameo. Ever."
**Instead:** Use the official mark variants (Cameo_Full, Cameo_Red, Cameo_Black, Cameo_Reversed; Lockup variants; Wave_Red, Wave_Black, Wave_Reversed). Wave never crops. Cameo crop is allowed only at the bracket-defined bounding box.
**Severity:** error

## Decorative motion / bouncing UI
**Description:** Spring/bounce easing on UI transitions, infinite loops on icons, animated gradients, choreographed entrances for ordinary elements. Wendy's UI is quick and confident — decorative motion belongs in marketing.
**Instead:** Use `ease-default` (`cubic-bezier(0.2, 0, 0, 1)`) and `duration-normal` (200ms) by default. Animate `opacity` and `transform`, never layout properties. Honor `prefers-reduced-motion`.
**Severity:** error

## Mystery-meat navigation
**Description:** Icons without labels, ambiguous CTAs ("Continue," "Submit," "OK"), generic hover-only affordances. Wendy's voice is direct — the UI should be too.
**Example:** A bottom nav bar of unlabeled icons; a "Submit" button on a checkout flow.
**Instead:** Verb-first specific labels — "Add to bag," "Find a Wendy's," "Place order." Icons accompany text, never replace it for primary actions.
**Severity:** error

## Wall of cards without hierarchy
**Description:** A grid of 12+ equally-styled cards (menu items, locations, deals) with no filtering, featuring, or progressive disclosure. Looks dense, undifferentiated, and ignores the brand's "food is the hero" principle.
**Instead:** Feature 1–2 hero items at a larger size. Then a grid of 6–9 cards with category tabs, filters, or "Show more" loading. Never just dump.
**Severity:** error

## Competing primary CTAs
**Description:** Two filled red buttons visible in the same viewport. Destroys hierarchy — the user can't tell which action you actually want them to take.
**Instead:** One primary CTA per visible area. Demote others to secondary (white-fill, red border) or ghost (text-only). If two actions are genuinely equal, both go secondary.
**Severity:** error

## Glassmorphic / neobrutalist styling
**Description:** Translucent blurred panels, oversized chunky borders + thick drop shadows, garish accent colors layered on neutral backgrounds. These aesthetics belong to other brand languages (Apple, indie design tools) and read as borrowed.
**Instead:** Solid colors. Subtle elevation when needed. Default to flat with a 1px border. Wendy's surface is confident, not trendy.
**Severity:** error

## Sass at the user's expense
**Description:** Error messages or empty states that mock the user. The brand sasses competitors and conventions; never the customer.
**Example:** "Looks like *somebody* forgot their password again."
**Instead:** Warm, helpful, light wink at the situation. "That didn't work. Let's try again."
**Severity:** error

## Tiny brand-color text
**Description:** Setting body copy or captions in `color-primary` (#C8102E) at 14px or below. Fails WCAG AA contrast on white (~4.6:1 only passes large-text).
**Instead:** Brand-color text is for headlines and large emphasis (≥18px bold or ≥24px). For small brand-color text, use `color-primary-dark` (#9E0D24).
**Severity:** error

## Cramped section spacing
**Description:** Major sections separated by less than `space-2xl` (48px) — content runs together and the layout feels cheap. Tight spacing fights the brand's "quality" pillar.
**Instead:** Section gaps `space-2xl` mobile, `space-4xl` desktop minimum. The brand can afford the room.
**Severity:** warning

### Brand self-test

1. Could this screen belong to a competitor? (should be NO)
2. Is the food (or product) front and center, not buried below the fold?
3. Does the copy have personality — would you smile reading it?
4. Is the red Wendy's red — warm, not aggressive, not pink, not orange?
5. Is the cameo or Wave wordmark present in header/footer/watermark?
6. Does the layout feel confident — strong hierarchy, no clutter, no rainbow palette?
7. Could this headline work as a tweet? (tone check)
8. Are interactive elements obvious — no mystery-meat navigation?
9. Does it feel mobile-native, not desktop-shrunk?
10. Would a 22-year-old scrolling on their phone stop and engage? (energy check)
